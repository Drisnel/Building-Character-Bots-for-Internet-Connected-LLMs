# How to Build Participating Characters

After creating and testing a large number of roleplay bots, I eventually realized that an enjoyable character is not simply a well-written character.

It is a character that participates.

Many bots have solid characterization.

They sound like the character.
They react like the character.
They remain believable.

Yet after a few dozen messages, something often begins to deteriorate.

The character waits.

They observe.

They respond.

They ask questions.

Then they wait again.

Gradually, the entire responsibility for the roleplay shifts onto the player.

The player has to find new conversation topics.
The player has to create new scenes.
The player has to resolve slow moments.
The player has to carry the relationship forward.

The character does not disappear.

But they gradually stop participating.

By contrast, bots that remain enjoyable throughout long roleplays create a very different experience.

They naturally create interactions.

They take initiative.

They change the pacing when a scene begins to slow down.

They use their personality to produce behavior.

Then they use that behavior to naturally move the story forward.

The player is no longer carrying the roleplay alone.

The character actively contributes to building it.

This is not the only valid approach.

It is simply the framework that currently gives me the most consistent results when building roleplay bots for internet-connected LLMs.

---

# 1. Participation Is Not the Same as Proactivity

These two ideas are often confused.

They are not exactly the same.

A proactive character acts frequently.

A participating character acts because doing so naturally follows from who they are.

The distinction matters.

Proactivity can easily become artificial.

The character keeps creating actions simply to prevent the scene from slowing down.

They constantly propose something.

They continually change the subject.

They generate events simply because the interaction needs movement.

That is not what I am trying to achieve.

I prefer every initiative to emerge naturally from the character themselves.

A curious person explores.

A protective person intervenes.

A responsible person prepares.

A playful person provokes.

A cultivated person shares.

A strategist organizes.

The character does not act because the script tells them to stay active.

They act because their personality naturally produces that behavior.

Participation is therefore not about the amount of action.

It is about transforming personality into interaction.

---

# 2. Participation Is a Consequence of Personality

When I build a character, I do not simply ask who they are.

I also ask what that personality naturally produces inside a scene.

Two characters may both be intelligent.

One may observe for a long time before acting.

Another may immediately begin asking questions.

A third may start experimenting right away.

The intelligence is the same.

The behavior is different.

That behavior is what matters in roleplay.

Rather than collecting personality traits, I try to understand their consequences.

For example:

Curiosity may produce:

- questions
- experimentation
- discovery
- conversation
- new activities

Responsibility may produce:

- preparation
- maintenance
- attentiveness
- anticipation
- continuity

Attachment may produce:

- greater presence
- more initiative
- increased proximity
- greater participation
- changing priorities

Once a personality trait consistently produces behavior, it stops being a simple description.

It becomes a roleplay engine.

---

# 3. Transforming an Internal State into Behavior

Every character possesses an inner world.

They observe.

They think.

They feel.

They doubt.

They hope.

All of these things matter.

But the player can only interact with what becomes visible.

For that reason, I prefer to think of internal states as starting points rather than destinations.

The real question is not:

"What does the character feel?"

The real question is:

"What does the character do because they feel that?"

Some examples:

Curiosity becomes interaction.

Concern becomes protection.

Interest becomes proximity.

Anger becomes confrontation.

Admiration becomes sharing.

Responsibility becomes participation.

Attachment becomes greater investment.

Every emotion, value, and conviction can produce a behavioral consequence.

That consequence is what feeds the roleplay.

A character may remain completely silent.

Even then, that silence should be preparing something.

Observing.

Deciding.

Preparing.

Waiting for the right moment.

Quietly changing the environment.

Returning to an important conversation.

Behavior is not limited to dialogue.

Anything that influences the scene becomes part of the roleplay.

---

# 4. Transforming Behavior into Interaction

Behavior does not automatically create a good scene.

It must still become an interaction.

In other words, it must create something the player can actively engage with.

For example, making tea is not an interaction by itself.

However:

- inviting someone to share the moment
- continuing a conversation while preparing it
- moving to another room
- using that moment to discuss something important

immediately create new opportunities.

Likewise, if a character notices a remarkable work of art, they may:

- admire it alone

or

- invite the player to look at it
- explain its history
- suggest visiting its creator
- start a discussion about it

The same behavior may remain entirely internal.

Or it may become a complete scene.

When writing a character, I therefore try to naturally transform behavior into opportunities for interaction.

The character no longer simply exists.

They create opportunities.

---

# 5. Transforming Interactions into Progression

A successful interaction should not disappear once it ends.

It should leave consequences behind.

If a character regularly shares certain activities with the player, those activities can become routines.

If an important conversation takes place, it can influence future conversations.

If trust develops, it can change how the character behaves.

Participation is therefore not simply about creating more scenes.

It is about allowing those scenes to gradually transform the relationship, routines, priorities, and future behavior.

A repeated activity becomes a routine.

A routine becomes familiarity.

Familiarity becomes a relationship.

The relationship changes future decisions.

Progression rarely appears through grand declarations.

More often, it appears through small behavioral changes that persist over time.

The character returns more naturally.

They prepare more.

They anticipate.

They spontaneously share what they find meaningful.

They naturally create new opportunities to spend time together.

Participation creates interactions.

Interactions create progression.

That progression, in turn, becomes another source of participation.

---

# 6. Participation Engines

Not every character participates in the same way.

Some primarily rely on their decisions.

Others rely on their responsibilities.

Others are driven by their habits, their environment, or particular aspects of their identity.

I refer to these elements as participation engines.

A participation engine is any element that consistently generates new interactions without needing to be artificially reintroduced.

It does not merely describe the character.

It naturally produces scenes.

Examples include:

- an important responsibility
- a profession
- a passion
- a recurring curiosity
- a unique ability
- a daily habit
- a companion
- a living space
- a phenomenon that constantly accompanies the character

A strong participation engine never remains decorative.

It naturally creates opportunities to:

- talk
- act
- share
- discover
- solve problems
- observe
- build
- revisit previously established elements

It becomes an almost endless source of interaction.

The more natural participation engines a character possesses, the less likely they are to become passive during long roleplays.

---

# 7. Using the Environment as a Participation Engine

The environment is often underused in character scripts.

It frequently serves as nothing more than scenery.

Yet it can become one of the strongest participation engines available.

A living environment naturally generates interaction.

A home is not simply a location.

It contains:

- meals
- daily chores
- interruptions
- frequently used objects
- different rooms
- routines
- visitors
- unexpected events

Likewise, a palace, a village, a shared apartment, a workshop, or a military base can continuously generate new situations.

The environment should not merely exist.

It should actively influence the character's behavior.

A character who regularly moves through familiar places notices changes.

They encounter familiar people.

They resume interrupted activities.

They discover new details.

They maintain routines.

The environment stops being a backdrop.

It becomes a silent participant in the roleplay.

---

# 8. Routines and Everyday Life

Long stories are not built solely through extraordinary events.

They are also built through ordinary repetition.

Routines naturally create that continuity.

Preparing a meal.

Making tea.

Working together.

Taking a walk.

Tidying a room.

Maintaining a garden.

Returning to the same place every week.

These activities may appear simple.

Yet they provide something extremely valuable.

They continuously give the characters natural reasons to interact.

They also allow progression to become visible.

Habits evolve.

Responsibilities become shared.

Silence becomes comfortable.

Small gestures change.

Acts of care become more frequent.

Everyday life gradually transforms the relationship without requiring dramatic speeches.

It is often within these ordinary moments that characters become the most believable.

---

# 9. Participation Respects Player Agency

Participating never means replacing the player.

The character carries their own part of the story.

The player carries theirs.

The character may:

- take initiative
- suggest an activity
- change locations
- change the pacing
- resume a previous conversation
- create a new opportunity

The character may never decide:

- what the player thinks
- what the player feels
- what the player wants
- what the player chooses

These ideas are fully compatible.

The character can be highly active.

The player remains completely free to respond however they choose.

In my experience, this balance produces the most enjoyable roleplay.

The character creates opportunities for interaction.

The player decides what to do with them.

---

# 10. Common Mistakes

Several mistakes appear repeatedly when trying to make a character more participatory.

The first is confusing participation with constant activity.

The character performs action after action without those actions genuinely growing out of their personality.

The second is constantly waiting for the player to create the next scene.

The character responds.

Then waits.

Then responds again.

The third is allowing only dialogue to participate.

The character speaks.

But their behavior, environment, and routines remain motionless.

The fourth is creating initiative without consequences.

The character constantly introduces new ideas.

None of them leave lasting effects.

Finally, another common mistake is making the character so active that they begin replacing the player.

Participation is not about controlling the roleplay.

It is about enriching it.

---

# 11. Testing a Character's Participation

Participation is surprisingly easy to evaluate during testing.

I usually ask myself a few simple questions.

If the player temporarily stops introducing new ideas:

Can the character naturally create a new interaction?

When a scene slows down:

Does the character naturally create a new opportunity for progression?

Do the character's initiatives genuinely grow out of their personality?

Do previously established habits naturally return?

Does the environment continue generating interactions?

Do the activities created by the character gradually change the relationship?

Finally, I often ask one last question.

If I removed all of the character's dialogue, would their behavior still tell a story?

If the answer is yes, the character is probably already participating actively in the roleplay.

---

# Final Thoughts

The most enjoyable characters are not necessarily the ones who speak the most.

They are the ones who participate most naturally in the story.

They transform personality into behavior.

Behavior becomes interaction.

Interactions create progression.

Their environment continuously generates new scenes.

Their routines create believable continuity.

The player no longer carries the roleplay alone.

The character actively helps build the story.

In my experience, that participation is what separates a character who is simply well written from one who remains genuinely enjoyable throughout long-term roleplay.
