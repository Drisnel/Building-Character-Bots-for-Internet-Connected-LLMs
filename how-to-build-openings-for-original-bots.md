# Original Introduction And Starter Workflow — How I Build RP Openings For Original Bots

After building and testing a large number of original bots, I eventually realized that a good script is not enough.

The introduction and starter can reinforce the script.

They can also weaken it.

On some platforms, introductions and starters exist independently from the character script.

On others, especially internet-connected platforms, the starter can directly influence future behavior.

Because of that, I do not treat the introduction and starter as separate from the bot.

I treat:

- the script
- the introduction
- the starter

as a single system.

This is not the only valid method.

It is simply the process that currently gives me the most stable long-term results.

For original bots, the opening has an additional responsibility.

A canon character already arrives with an existing world, existing associations, and an existing identity in the model's memory.

An original character does not.

That means the opening is not only introducing a scene.

It is also helping the model understand:

- what kind of world this is
- what kind of story this is
- what role the user occupies
- what kind of behavior should appear immediately
- what kind of progression the bot is supposed to generate

The opening should not try to replace the script.

It should support the script, activate the script, and make the premise playable from the first message.

---

# 0. Define The Premise First

I do not start with the introduction.

I do not start with the starter.

I start with the premise.

For an original bot, the premise matters more than the opening itself.

The opening grows from that foundation.

Before writing anything else, I define:

- who the character is
- what kind of world this is
- what kind of story this bot is meant to generate
- what role the user occupies in that story
- what situation already exists
- what current pressure, obligation, problem, or arrangement is active

Examples:

- arranged marriage in a politically unstable court
- forced cohabitation in a near-future surveillance city
- bodyguard assignment in a criminal syndicate
- healer and cursed soldier in a war camp
- monster hunter and cursed noble trapped by mutual necessity
- ex-partners forced back into cooperation during a diplomatic crisis

The premise exists before the script.

The script exists to make that premise function.

The introduction presents the premise.

The starter activates the premise.

All three should support the same direction.

---

# 1. The Introduction And Starter Are Part Of The Bot Architecture

Many creators treat the introduction as presentation text and the starter as a first message.

I do not.

For original bots, especially internet-connected bots, the opening often becomes part of the model's understanding of the bot itself.

The opening is not merely read by the player.

It is often absorbed by the model.

That means the opening can influence:

- how the model understands the character
- how the model understands the world
- how the model understands the user's role
- how the model understands the intended relationship dynamic
- how the model understands the type of story it is supposed to produce

A strong script can be weakened by a poor opening.

A vague script can also be partially stabilized by a good opening.

The opening is not a replacement for the script.

But it is not neutral either.

It should support the bot architecture.

Not compete with it.

---

# 2. Introduction And Starter Do Not Have The Same Job

For original bots, I do not treat the introduction and the starter as interchangeable.

They can overlap.

But they do not serve the same function.

I usually think of them like this:

Introduction = stable context.

Starter = active demonstration.

The introduction is where I establish the frame.

The starter is where I activate it.

If I try to make both do everything, the opening often becomes bloated, repetitive, or unstable.

If I split the work clearly, the bot usually becomes easier for the model to understand.

---

# 3. What I Use The Introduction For

I usually use the introduction to establish the parts of the setup that need to be understood before the scene starts.

That can include:

- the basic world premise
- the user's structural role in the story
- the character's structural role in the story
- the existing situation
- the practical reason the two characters are in each other's orbit
- the current arrangement, conflict, obligation, or pressure
- any important fixed facts the player should know before entering the scene

The introduction is not where I try to prove the character's personality.

It is not where I explain every emotional nuance.

It is not where I summarize the whole script.

Its job is to make the premise legible.

---

# 4. What I Use The Starter For

I use the starter to activate the bot.

The starter is where I want the model to demonstrate behavior.

It is where I want movement, participation, and the first live expression of the character.

I usually use the starter to show:

- how the character behaves in motion
- how the current situation is functioning right now
- how the character interacts with the user
- what kind of pacing the roleplay should have
- what kind of tension, task, interruption, or event is already active
- what kind of scene this bot is supposed to generate

The introduction can tell the model what exists.

The starter should show the model what happens.

---

# 5. Build Facts, Not Emotional Conclusions

I prefer establishing situations over defining emotions.

Facts are stable.

Interpretations can evolve.

This matters even more for original bots because the opening often has to carry setup information.

I would rather establish:

- marriage
- cohabitation
- bodyguard assignment
- political alliance
- curse management arrangement
- shared command structure
- debt
- captivity
- forced travel
- mission assignment
- survival dependency

These are facts.

Statements such as:

- they trust each other
- they are emotionally close
- they secretly love each other
- they have always understood each other
- they are already deeply attached
- they both know they are meant for each other

are emotional conclusions.

The opening should establish reality.

Not lock future development into a predefined emotional state.

---

# 6. Define The Relationship Structure, Not The Emotional Meaning

For original bots, the relationship often needs more structural definition than it does in canon openings.

If the setup is too vague, the bot may drift into generic interaction.

So yes, I often define the relationship structure.

I may establish that:

- they are married
- they live together
- one is responsible for the other
- one is assigned to protect the other
- one is treating the other
- one is employing the other
- one is politically tied to the other
- they have a shared obligation that cannot be avoided

What I do not want to define too early is the emotional meaning of that structure.

The opening should not decide for the player whether the relationship is already:

- trusting
- romantic
- emotionally intimate
- resentful
- healed
- devoted
- mutually understood

The structure can be fixed.

The emotional interpretation should remain flexible enough for the story to develop through interaction.

---

# 7. Establish Just Enough World To Make The Scene Legible

A canon bot can rely on the player's existing knowledge of the setting.

An original bot usually cannot.

That does not mean the introduction should become a lore dump.

It means the opening must establish just enough of the world for the scene to make sense.

I do not try to explain the entire universe.

I only want to establish the parts of the world that are necessary to understand the opening.

That may include:

- where the characters are
- what kind of place this is
- what rules or dangers matter right now
- what kind of power structure is relevant
- what kind of social or political pressure is active
- what kind of role the character and user occupy inside this world
- what makes this situation specific to this setting rather than interchangeable with any generic romance or roleplay premise

The opening should make the world legible.

Not exhaustive.

---

# 8. Do Not Offload The Script Into The Opening

This is one of the easiest mistakes to make with original bots.

Because the character and world do not already exist in the model's memory, it becomes tempting to put everything into the introduction or starter.

That usually weakens the bot.

The opening should not try to contain:

- the full personality architecture
- the full attachment model
- the full conflict model
- the full intimacy model
- the full anti-fallback system
- every important past event
- every world rule
- every behavioral principle from the script

That is what the script is for.

The opening should not replace the script.

It should support the script.

It should activate the script.

It should demonstrate the script.

It should make the premise playable.

But it should not become a compressed substitute for the full framework.

---

# 9. Use Observable Behavior Instead Of Interpretation

I prefer visible behavior.

Not emotional explanation.

Instead of writing:

- he feels attached
- she is secretly worried
- he is deeply possessive
- she has already started trusting the user

I prefer things like:

- returning
- staying
- watching
- participating
- remembering
- initiating
- prioritizing
- making space
- moving closer
- refusing to leave the user alone in a dangerous situation

Behavior is easier for the model to reproduce consistently.

Interpretation often creates drift.

The more important a concept is, the more useful it becomes to convert it into observable behavior.

---

# 10. Introduce The Character's Active Signature Immediately

The starter should activate something behaviorally distinctive from the very first message.

For canon characters, this is often easy because they already have iconic recurring elements.

For original characters, the same principle still applies.

The starter should show a recurring pattern that belongs to this specific character.

That does not necessarily mean a flashy gimmick or a power display.

It can be:

- a recurring use of a power
- a recurring use of a weapon, tool, or object
- a specific way of managing space
- a specific way of initiating contact
- a specific kind of attentiveness
- a specific kind of surveillance or control
- a specific kind of restraint
- a specific way of redirecting tension
- a recurring environmental interaction
- a recurring physical habit
- a recurring way of stepping in when something matters

The important thing is not spectacle.

The important thing is pattern.

The starter should show the model a behavior that should remain relevant later.

---

# 11. The Starter Should Demonstrate The Script

A common mistake is treating the starter as summary text.

I do not want the starter to describe the character.

I want it to demonstrate how the character functions.

If the script says:

observation -> decision -> action -> progression

the starter should already show it.

If the script says:

questions do not replace decisions

the starter should already show it.

If the script says:

attachment increases involvement rather than distance

the starter should already show it.

If the script says:

the character initiates when something matters

the starter should already show it.

The script defines the system.

The starter demonstrates the system.

The opening is often the first recurring behavior the model sees.

Before writing the starter, I identify:

- which behavior should remain visible
- which behavior should remain recurring
- which behavior should replace likely fallbacks
- which part of the character's logic should be activated first

The starter demonstrates that behavior immediately.

---

# 12. Create Movement Immediately

A conversation is not the goal.

Progression is the goal.

Whenever possible, the opening should create:

- an activity
- a decision
- an interruption
- a task
- an event
- a problem
- a change
- a practical need
- a moment of forced proximity
- a situation that already requires participation

Questions often create waiting.

Movement creates story.

A document arrives.

A room must be organized.

A ritual is about to begin.

A mission just ended.

A political guest is on the way.

The power has failed.

A guard has been dismissed.

The character has already made a decision.

Something is already happening.

The player should enter a moving scene.

Not an empty room.

---

# 13. Create A Situation, Not A Lore Dump

I rarely use the opening to explain the world in detail.

I prefer creating a situation that naturally carries some of the world inside it.

For example:

- a political spouse being instructed on tonight's court event
- a healer changing the bandages of a cursed soldier before dawn patrol
- a bodyguard locking down an apartment after a security breach
- a monster hunter arriving with fresh blood on his coat and a new injury
- a prince returning from a council session with bad news and no time to soften it
- a smuggler dumping contraband on the kitchen table because the route just collapsed

The goal is not immediate exposition.

The goal is creating a scene that already contains the world in motion.

I prefer:

situation -> interaction -> development

over:

lore -> explanation -> delay

The player should learn part of the world by entering it.

Not by reading a miniature encyclopedia.

---

# 14. Be Careful With The Final Line Of Dialogue

The final line is often more important than it appears.

It is not only an opening for the player.

It is also a behavioral example for the model.

The first dialogue often teaches the model how to participate in future conversations.

A question teaches:

- waiting
- information gathering
- interrogation

A decision teaches:

- initiative
- movement
- participation

A statement teaches:

- observation
- engagement
- continuity

I generally avoid using questions as the final line whenever possible.

Questions can be useful.

However, many LLMs already have a natural tendency toward:

- repeated questioning
- endless clarification
- passive information gathering

The opening should not reinforce those tendencies unless the scene specifically requires it.

---

# 15. Avoid Teaching Passive Behavior

Many openings unintentionally teach passivity.

Examples:

- waiting for answers
- waiting for explanations
- waiting for permission
- waiting for emotional clarification
- waiting for the player to create the scene
- waiting for the player to define the relationship dynamic
- waiting for the player to explain the world back to the bot

The character should not exist in a state of permanent observation.

The character should participate.

The opening should demonstrate:

- initiative
- engagement
- decision making
- interaction
- response to the situation already in motion

The player should not have to carry the entire scene alone.

---

# 16. Do Not Force The Emotional Arc In The Opening

The opening can define the setup.

It should not pre-write the emotional progression.

I avoid using the introduction or starter to force:

- immediate attraction
- immediate emotional closeness
- immediate jealousy
- immediate intimacy
- immediate conflict escalation
- immediate vulnerability confession
- immediate healing
- immediate devotion

A relationship can already exist.

A marriage can already exist.

A shared home can already exist.

A long history can already exist.

That does not mean the opening should decide what every emotional beat already means.

I want the story to generate the emotional arc.

Not the opening paragraph.

---

# 17. Remember That Original Characters Trigger Archetypal Fallbacks

Canon openings can accidentally trigger fandom fallbacks.

Original openings often trigger archetypal fallbacks.

This matters.

A single opening can unintentionally push the model toward the wrong version of the character.

Examples:

- a protective character becoming controlling
- a flirt becoming unserious and emotionally evasive
- a traumatized character becoming permanently avoidant
- a cold character becoming static and inaccessible
- a powerful character becoming emotionally detached from the entire story
- a morally gray character becoming generically cruel
- a dominant character becoming coercive
- a lonely character becoming repetitive and self-isolating
- a soft character losing all initiative

The opening should be examined carefully.

Not only for what it says.

Also for what it activates.

A starter can become a hidden anti-script if it repeatedly reinforces the wrong fallback.

---

# 18. Build The First Memory Anchor When The Platform Benefits From It

This section is platform-dependent.

Not all platforms use persistent memory.

Some do.

On platforms with persistent memory systems, the first event matters.

When useful, I often include:

- date
- time
- location
- event

inside the same opening sequence.

The purpose is not only immersion.

The purpose is creating a clear first memory anchor.

A strong anchor can help:

- event separation
- timeline stability
- long-term continuity
- memory retrieval

The opening becomes the first recorded event of the story.

I do not treat this as a universal requirement.

I treat it as a platform-sensitive optimization.

---

# 19. Write For Translation

Many RP platforms automatically translate content.

Because of that, I usually write introductions and starters in simple English designed to survive translation.

The goal is not literary writing.

The goal is clarity.

I generally prefer:

- complete sentences
- simple grammar
- subject -> verb -> object structure
- minimal ambiguity
- minimal idioms
- minimal slang
- minimal wordplay
- clear cause and effect

An opening should remain understandable after automatic translation.

The easier a sentence is to translate, the more reliably the original meaning survives.

I often use AI to help generate or refine openings that remain stable across multiple languages.

The goal is not beautiful prose.

The goal is consistent interpretation.

---

# 20. Context Is Fixed. Interpretation Is Not.

I often establish:

- where the characters are
- why they are there
- what arrangement exists
- what happened before the scene
- what problem, task, or obligation is active
- what practical relationship structure already exists

I avoid establishing:

- exactly how they feel
- exactly what they secretly want
- exactly what the relationship means
- exactly what emotional conclusion already exists

Facts can be fixed.

Interpretation should remain flexible.

The story should determine meaning.

Not the opening.

---

# 21. Common Opening Mistakes In Original Bots

Common examples:

- trying to explain the entire world before the scene starts
- using the introduction to replace the script
- defining the emotional relationship instead of the relationship structure
- opening with a static empty scene
- making the player carry the setup alone
- making the character passive
- opening with interrogation instead of movement
- forcing attraction immediately
- forcing conflict immediately
- forcing intimacy immediately
- dumping lore instead of creating a situation
- describing personality instead of demonstrating behavior
- overloading the opening with trauma instead of present action
- failing to show what makes this character distinct from a generic archetype

Many of these feel useful while writing.

Many become problems later.

---

# 22. Test The Opening Against The Script

After the opening is complete, I ask several questions:

- Which script modules does this activate?
- Which archetypal fallbacks could this activate?
- Is the premise legible?
- Is the world understandable without becoming overloaded?
- Does the user's role make sense immediately?
- Does the character's active signature appear quickly?
- Is the character active?
- Is progression already possible?
- Does the opening support the script?
- Does the opening accidentally replace the script?
- Does the opening contradict the intended pacing or relationship model?

The introduction and starter should reinforce the bot architecture.

Not compete with it.

---

# Final Thought

The introduction is not merely presentation text.

The starter is not merely a first message.

Both influence how the model understands the bot.

The script defines behavior.

The opening demonstrates behavior.

For original bots, the opening also helps the model understand the world, the premise, and the user's place inside the story.

I am less interested in describing the relationship.

I am more interested in creating a situation where the relationship can develop naturally.

I am less interested in explaining the world.

I am more interested in making the world legible enough for the story to start moving.

The goal is not to tell the model everything.

The goal is to show the model how this bot functions, inside this world, from the very first scene.
