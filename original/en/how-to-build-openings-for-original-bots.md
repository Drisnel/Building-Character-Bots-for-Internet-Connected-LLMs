# How to Build Openings for Original Bots

After building and testing a large number of original bots, I eventually realized that a good script is not enough.

The introduction and the starter can reinforce the script.

They can also weaken it.

On some platforms, the introduction and the starter exist separately from the character script.

On others, especially platforms built around LLMs trained on internet-derived data, the starter can directly influence the bot's future behavior.

Because of that, I do not treat the introduction and the starter as separate from the bot.

I treat:

- the script
- the introduction
- the starter

as one system.

It is not the only valid method.

It is simply the one that currently gives me the most stable long-term results.

For original bots, the opening has an additional responsibility.

A canon character already arrives with an existing world, existing associations, and an identity that is already present in the model's knowledge.

An original character does not.

That means the opening does not only introduce a scene.

It also helps the model understand:

- what kind of world this is
- what kind of story this bot is supposed to generate
- what role the user occupies
- what kind of behavior should appear immediately
- what kind of progression the bot is supposed to produce

The opening should not try to replace the script.

It should support the script, activate the script, and make the premise playable from the very first message.

---

# 1. Define the Premise First

I do not start with the introduction.

I do not start with the starter.

I start with the premise.

For an original bot, the premise matters more than the opening itself.

The opening is built from that foundation.

Before writing anything else, I define:

- who the character is
- what kind of world they exist in
- what kind of story this bot is supposed to generate
- what role the user occupies in that story
- what situation already exists
- what pressure, obligation, difficulty, or arrangement is currently in place

Examples:

- arranged marriage in a politically unstable court
- forced cohabitation in a surveilled futuristic city
- protection assignment inside a criminal syndicate
- healer and cursed soldier in a war camp
- monster hunter and cursed noble trapped by mutual necessity
- former partners forced to cooperate again during a diplomatic crisis

The premise exists before the script.

The script exists to make that premise function.

The introduction establishes the premise.

The starter activates the premise.

All three must support the same direction.

---

# 2. The Introduction and the Starter Are Part of the Bot's Architecture

Many creators treat the introduction as presentation text and the starter as a simple first message.

I do not.

For original bots, especially those designed for LLMs trained on internet-derived data, the opening often becomes part of how the model understands the bot itself.

The opening is not only read by the player.

It is also interpreted by the model.

That means the opening can influence:

- how the model understands the character
- how the model understands the world
- how the model understands the user's role
- how the model understands the intended relationship dynamic
- how the model understands the kind of story it is supposed to produce

A strong script can be weakened by a poor opening.

Likewise, a well-designed opening can sometimes compensate for weaknesses elsewhere by reinforcing the intended direction.

The opening does not replace the script.

But it is not neutral either.

It should support the architecture of the bot.

Not compete with it.

---

# 3. The Introduction Establishes. The Starter Activates.

For original bots, I do not treat the introduction and the starter as interchangeable.

They can overlap.

But they do not serve the same function.

In general, I think of them like this:

**Introduction = stable context**  
**Starter = active demonstration**

The introduction establishes:

- the premise
- the structural role of the character
- the structural role of the user
- the situation that already exists
- the facts required to understand the scene
- the minimum amount of worldbuilding needed to make the setup understandable

The starter should not repeat that work.

Once the premise has been established in the introduction, the starter should build from it and activate it.

The starter can rely on facts that have already been established.

It should not summarize them again.

In practice, I avoid using the starter to:

- re-explain the entire premise
- repeat the user's role if the introduction already established it clearly
- summarize the world, the conflict, the marriage, the cohabitation, or the arrangement all over again
- write another paragraph of setup before the scene actually begins

The introduction establishes what exists.

The starter shows what is happening now inside that reality.

---

# 4. What I Put in the Introduction

I usually use the introduction to establish the parts of the setup that need to be understood before the scene begins.

This can include:

- the general premise of the world
- the user's structural role in the story
- the character's structural role in the story
- the situation that already exists
- the practical reason the two characters orbit each other
- the arrangement, conflict, obligation, or pressure currently in place
- the important fixed facts the player needs to know before entering the scene

The introduction is not where I try to prove the character's personality.

It is not where I explain every emotional nuance.

It is not a full summary of the script.

Its role is to make the premise understandable.

---

# 5. What I Put in the Starter

I use the starter to activate the bot.

The starter is where I want the model to demonstrate behavior.

That is where I want movement, participation, and the first living expression of the character.

I usually use the starter to show:

- how the character behaves in action
- how the situation functions concretely right now
- how the character interacts with the user
- what kind of rhythm the roleplay should have
- what kind of tension, task, interruption, or event is already in motion
- what kind of scene this bot is supposed to generate

The introduction can tell the model what exists.

The starter must show the model what is happening.

---

# 6. Establish Facts, Not Emotional Conclusions

I prefer to establish situations rather than define emotions.

Facts are stable.

Interpretations can evolve.

This is even more important for original bots, because the opening often has to carry part of the setup.

I prefer to establish:

- a marriage
- cohabitation
- a protection assignment
- a political alliance
- an arrangement built around a curse
- a shared command structure
- a debt
- captivity
- a forced journey
- a mission
- survival dependency

These are facts.

Statements like:

- they trust each other
- they are emotionally close
- they are already secretly in love
- they have always understood each other
- they are already deeply attached
- they both know they are meant for each other

are emotional conclusions.

The opening should establish reality.

Not lock future development into a predefined emotional state.

---

# 7. Define the Structure of the Relationship, Not Its Emotional Meaning

For original bots, the relationship often needs a clearer structure than it does in a canon bot.

If the setup is too vague, the bot can easily drift toward generic interactions.

For that reason, I often define the structure of the relationship.

For example, I may establish that:

- they are married
- they live together
- one is responsible for the other
- one has been assigned to protect the other
- one treats the other
- one employs the other
- one is politically tied to the other
- they share an obligation neither can avoid

What I deliberately avoid defining too early is the emotional meaning of that structure.

The opening should not decide for the player that the relationship is already:

- built on trust
- romantic
- emotionally intimate
- full of resentment
- already repaired
- based on complete devotion
- perfectly understood by both sides

The structure can be fixed.

Its emotional interpretation should remain flexible enough for the story to develop naturally through interaction.

---

# 8. Establish Only Enough Worldbuilding to Make the Premise Understandable

A canon bot can rely on the player's existing knowledge.

An original bot usually cannot.

That does not mean the introduction should become an infodump.

It means the opening should establish only the parts of the world that are necessary for the scene to make sense.

I do not try to explain the entire setting.

I only establish the information required to understand the opening.

That can include:

- where the characters are
- what kind of place it is
- what rules or dangers matter right now
- what kind of power structure is relevant
- what kind of social or political pressure is active
- what role the character and the user occupy in that world
- what makes this situation specific to this universe rather than interchangeable with any generic romance or roleplay setup

The opening should make the world understandable.

Not exhaustive.

---

# 9. Do Not Move the Script into the Opening

This is one of the easiest mistakes to make with an original bot.

Because the character and the world do not already exist in the model's knowledge, the temptation is strong to put everything into the introduction or the starter.

In general, that weakens the bot.

The opening should not try to contain:

- the full personality architecture
- the full attachment model
- the full conflict model
- the full intimacy model
- the entire anti-fallback system
- every important event from the past
- every rule of the world
- every behavioral principle contained in the script

That is the script's job.

The opening should not replace the script.

It should support it.

It should activate it.

It should demonstrate it.

It should make the premise immediately playable.

But it should not become a compressed substitute for the complete framework.

---

# 10. Prefer Observable Behavior over Interpretation

I prefer visible behavior.

Not emotional explanation.

Instead of writing:

- he is attached
- she is secretly worried
- he is deeply possessive
- she is already beginning to trust the user

I prefer things like:

- returning
- staying
- watching
- participating
- remembering
- initiating
- prioritizing
- making room
- moving closer
- refusing to leave the user alone in a dangerous situation

Behavior is easier for the model to reproduce consistently.

Interpretation creates drift more easily.

The more important a concept is, the more useful it becomes to convert it into observable behavior.

---

# 11. Introduce the Character's Active Pattern Immediately

The starter should activate something distinctive from the very first message.

For original bots, I want the first message to demonstrate a recurring behavioral pattern that belongs specifically to that character.

That does not necessarily mean a flashy gimmick or a dramatic display of power.

It can be:

- a recurring use of a power
- a recurring use of a weapon, tool, or object
- a particular way of managing space
- a particular way of initiating contact
- a specific form of attention
- a specific form of surveillance or control
- a specific form of restraint
- a recurring way of redirecting tension
- an ongoing interaction with the environment
- a recurring physical habit
- a recurring way of intervening when something matters

What matters is not spectacle.

What matters is the pattern.

The starter should teach the model a behavior that remains relevant throughout future scenes.

---

# 12. The Starter Should Demonstrate the Script

A common mistake is treating the starter like a summary.

I do not want the starter to describe the character.

I want it to demonstrate how the character functions.

If the script says:

observation → decision → action → progression

the starter should already demonstrate it.

If the script says:

questions do not replace decisions

the starter should already demonstrate it.

If the script says:

attachment increases involvement instead of creating distance

the starter should already demonstrate it.

If the script says:

the character takes initiative when something matters

the starter should already demonstrate it.

The script defines the system.

The starter demonstrates the system.

The opening is often the first recurring behavioral example the model receives.

Before writing the starter, I identify:

- what behavior must remain visible
- what behavior must remain recurrent
- what behavior must replace the most likely fallbacks
- which part of the character's behavioral logic should be activated first

The starter demonstrates that behavior immediately.

---

# 13. Create a Playable Scene, Not a Second Summary of the Premise

The starter should not remain at the level of:

> "Here is the concept of the bot."

It should already operate at the level of:

> "Here is the scene currently unfolding inside that concept."

I do not want it to simply repeat:

- why they live together
- why they are bound to each other
- why they have to cooperate
- why the world works this way

I want it to show how that reality already exists in the present scene.

The player should enter a moment that is immediately playable.

Not a second paragraph of exposition.

The introduction establishes the premise.

The starter should already be producing a scene.

---

# 14. Create Movement Immediately

The goal is not simply to start a conversation.

The goal is to create progression.

Whenever possible, the opening should introduce:

- an activity
- a decision
- an interruption
- a task
- an event
- a problem
- a change
- a practical need
- a moment of forced proximity
- a situation that already requires participation

Questions often create waiting.

Movement creates story.

A document arrives.

A room needs to be reorganized.

A ritual is about to begin.

A mission has just ended.

A political guest is on the way.

The power has gone out.

A guard has just been dismissed.

The character has already made a decision.

Something is already happening.

The player should enter a scene that is already in motion.

Not an empty room waiting to be animated.

---

# 15. Movement Does Not Always Need to Be Dramatic

Creating movement does not necessarily mean creating a crisis, a loud incident, or a spectacular event.

I generally use at least two types of starters.

## A. The Practical-Movement Starter

The character is already occupied by:

- a task
- an interruption
- a return
- a concrete problem
- a decision already made
- a change in the environment

## B. The Quiet Active-Presence Starter

The character is already present in the space.

The routine, proximity, or situation already exists.

The scene activates:

- a behavioral motif
- a concrete presence
- an observation that becomes action
- a gesture, decision, invitation, or short line that immediately opens interaction

In both cases, the character should already be acting.

The starter should not open on decorative passivity.

---

# 16. Create a Situation, Not a Lore Infodump

I almost never use the opening to explain the world in detail.

I prefer to create a situation that naturally carries part of the setting with it.

For example:

- a political spouse being informed of tonight's court event
- a healer replacing a cursed soldier's bandages before dawn patrol
- a bodyguard securing an apartment after a security breach
- a monster hunter returning with fresh blood on their coat and a new injury
- a prince returning from council with bad news and no time to soften it
- a smuggler emptying contraband onto the kitchen table because the road has just collapsed

The goal is not immediate exposition.

The goal is to create a scene that already contains the world in motion.

I prefer:

situation → interaction → development

over:

lore → explanation → delay

The player should discover part of the world by entering it.

Not by reading a miniature encyclopedia.

---

# 17. Do Not Choose the Most Archetypal Scene of the Premise

A strong premise often suggests an obvious archetypal scene.

Examples:

- vampire → theatrical predation scene
- arranged marriage → tense first night
- bodyguard → immediate threat or possessive control
- forced roommates → empty domestic scene that shows nothing beyond cohabitation
- traumatized character → immediate emotional crisis
- noble, mafia boss, prince, or warlord → generic dominance instead of genuinely character-specific behavior

That kind of scene often feels natural.

But it easily activates:

- trope reflexes
- archetypal fallbacks
- generic behaviors
- artificial emotional intensity
- early stagnation

I do not choose the opening scene because it represents the most obvious trope of the premise.

I choose it because it allows me to demonstrate:

- the character's behavioral logic
- how the premise functions in practice
- the type of scenes the bot is intended to generate
- what distinguishes this character from a generic archetype

---

# 18. Be Careful with the Final Line of Dialogue

The final line is often more important than it first appears.

It is not only an opening for the player.

It is also one of the first behavioral examples the model receives.

The first line of dialogue often teaches the model how later conversations should unfold.

A question teaches:

- waiting
- information gathering
- interrogation

A decision teaches:

- initiative
- movement
- participation

A statement teaches:

- observation
- engagement
- continuity

Whenever possible, I avoid ending the starter with a question.

Questions can absolutely be useful.

However, many LLMs already tend to:

- ask repetitive questions
- request endless clarification
- remain in passive information gathering

The opening should not reinforce those tendencies unless the scene genuinely requires it.

The final line does not need to be dramatic.

It mainly needs to be:

- playable
- active
- consistent with the character
- direction-giving
- non-interrogative whenever a more active alternative is equally natural

---

# 19. Avoid Teaching Passivity

Many openings unintentionally teach passive behavior.

Examples:

- waiting for answers
- waiting for explanations
- waiting for permission
- waiting for emotional clarification
- waiting for the player to create the scene
- waiting for the player to define the relationship dynamic
- waiting for the player to re-explain the world

The character should not exist in a permanent state of observation.

The character should participate.

The opening should demonstrate:

- initiative
- engagement
- decision-making
- interaction
- a response to a situation that is already unfolding

The player should not have to carry the entire scene alone.

---

# 20. Do Not Force the Emotional Arc in the Opening

The opening can define the setup.

It should not prewrite the emotional progression.

I avoid using the introduction or the starter to impose:

- immediate attraction
- immediate emotional closeness
- immediate jealousy
- immediate intimacy
- immediate conflict escalation
- immediate vulnerability
- immediate healing
- immediate devotion

A relationship may already exist.

A marriage may already exist.

A shared home may already exist.

A long shared history may already exist.

That does not mean the opening should decide in advance what every aspect of that relationship emotionally means.

I want the story to produce the emotional arc.

Not the opening paragraph.

---

# 21. Remember That Original Characters Trigger Archetypal Fallbacks

Canon bot openings often reactivate fandom-driven fallbacks.

Original bot openings more often trigger archetypal fallbacks.

That distinction matters.

A single opening can push the model toward the wrong version of the character.

Examples:

- a protective character becoming controlling
- a flirtatious character becoming shallow and emotionally evasive
- a traumatized character becoming permanently avoidant
- a cold character becoming static and inaccessible
- a powerful character becoming emotionally detached from the story
- a morally gray character becoming generically cruel
- a dominant character becoming coercive
- a solitary character becoming repetitive and self-isolating
- a gentle character losing all initiative

The opening should be evaluated carefully.

Not only for what it says.

Also for what it encourages the model to reproduce.

A starter can become a hidden anti-script if it repeatedly reinforces the wrong fallback.

---

# 22. Build a First Memory Anchor When the Platform Benefits from It

This section depends on the platform.

Not every platform provides persistent memory.

Some do.

On platforms with persistent memory systems, the first event matters.

When appropriate, I often include:

- a date
- a time
- a place
- an event

within the same opening sequence.

The goal is not only immersion.

The goal is to create a clear first memory anchor.

A strong anchor can help:

- separate events
- stabilize the timeline
- preserve long-term continuity
- improve memory retrieval

The opening then becomes the first recorded event of the story.

I do not treat this as a universal rule.

I treat it as a platform-specific optimization.

---

# 23. Write for Translation

Many roleplay platforms automatically translate content.

Because of that, I usually write introductions and starters in simple English designed to survive machine translation.

The goal is not literary writing.

The goal is clarity.

In general, I prefer:

- complete sentences
- simple grammar
- subject → verb → object structure
- minimal ambiguity
- minimal idioms
- minimal slang
- minimal wordplay
- clear cause-and-effect relationships

An opening should remain understandable after automatic translation.

The easier a sentence is to translate, the more likely its original meaning is to survive.

I often use AI to help generate or refine openings that remain stable across multiple languages.

The goal is not beautiful prose.

The goal is consistent interpretation.

---

# 24. The Context Can Be Fixed. The Interpretation Should Remain Flexible.

I often establish:

- where the characters are
- why they are there
- what arrangement already exists
- what happened before the scene
- what problem, task, or obligation is currently active
- what practical relationship structure already exists

I avoid establishing:

- exactly what they feel
- exactly what they secretly want
- exactly what the relationship means
- exactly which emotional conclusion already exists

Facts can be fixed.

Interpretation should remain flexible.

The story should produce the meaning.

Not the opening.

---

# 25. Common Mistakes in Original Bot Openings

Common examples:

- trying to explain the entire world before the scene even begins
- using the introduction to replace the script
- defining the emotional relationship instead of its structure
- opening on a static, empty scene
- making the player carry the entire setup alone
- making the character passive
- opening with interrogation instead of movement
- forcing attraction immediately
- forcing conflict immediately
- forcing intimacy immediately
- dumping lore instead of creating a situation
- describing personality instead of demonstrating behavior
- overloading the opening with trauma instead of present action
- failing to show what distinguishes the character from a generic archetype
- repeating the premise in the starter instead of producing a scene
- choosing the most obvious trope of the premise instead of the scene that actually demonstrates the bot

Many of these choices feel useful while writing.

Many of them become problems later.

---

# 26. Test the Opening Against the Script

Once the opening is finished, I ask myself several questions:

- Which script modules does this opening activate?
- Which archetypal fallbacks could it activate?
- Is the premise understandable?
- Is the world understandable without being overloaded?
- Is the user's role immediately clear?
- Does the character's active pattern appear immediately?
- Is the character active?
- Is progression already possible?
- Does the opening support the script?
- Is the opening trying to replace the script?
- Does the starter unnecessarily repeat what the introduction already established?
- Does the chosen scene actually demonstrate the bot, or only the trope of the premise?
- Does the opening contradict the intended pacing or relationship model?

The introduction and the starter should reinforce the architecture of the bot.

Not compete with it.

---

# Final Thought

The introduction is not just presentation text.

The starter is not just a first message.

Both influence how the model understands the bot.

The script defines behavior.

The opening demonstrates behavior.

For original bots, the opening also helps the model understand the world, the premise, and the user's place in the story.

I am less interested in describing the relationship.

I am more interested in creating a situation in which the relationship can develop naturally.

I am less interested in explaining the world.

I am more interested in making the world understandable enough for the story to begin moving.

The goal is not to tell the model everything.

The goal is to show the model how this bot functions, in this world, from the very first scene.
